---
title: Recalling the days of working in Beijing in 2023
description: Fragments and reflections on work and life in Beijing in 2023.
---

Those days in Beijing in 2023, looking back now, are like a roll of developed film—the images remain, but the edges have yellowed slightly, with a sense of distance and a touch of nostalgia.

## Arriving in Beijing

I decided to come to Beijing that year, mostly for the opportunities and industry concentration. Another reason was that I had been to Beijing as a child, when my parents worked there, and I could spend summers and winters with them for a short time.

I officially arrived in Beijing during graduation season. On the afternoon of June 15th, I got off at the South Station, rented a room, and the landlord—a woman from Shandong who had married into the area—was very kind and chatted with me a bit more when she learned I was from the same province.

Later I moved to another place in Longshan Jiayuan—a resettlement housing complex run by Ziru. The room was tiny, less than six square meters.

The day after renting, I reported to the company. Not long after, I bought an electric scooter. It served me well and is still in use at my father's place.

## Work and daily life

What I remember from work now are mostly trivial things—what seemed huge at the time feels like a grain of sand in time and space when I look back.

The company was first in an industrial park in Shijingshan, then moved to the former Shougang site. In Shijingshan, we often had ribs for lunch; when I first arrived in Beijing, they tasted amazing. Later my palate became pickier, and now I realize it was probably pre-made food. But back then, just starting out, the satisfaction was something special.

After moving to Shougang Park in Shijingshan, lunch was often at a knife-cut noodle shop—the cheapest option. Sometimes we ate downstairs, sometimes ordered delivery. I remember once ordering from that same noodle shop and getting Kung Pao Chicken—they literally dumped the dish into the box like leftovers. I later learned the chef thought it was for dine-in and then transferred it to a takeout container. I left a big bad review; Kung Pao Chicken dumped like that really did look like pig feed.

I almost never took the subway in Beijing—I preferred riding my electric scooter. Winter was rough; it was bitterly cold and I never bought proper gloves. A thin pair got me through the whole season. To keep the wind out, I sometimes wore a black plastic bag over my hands.

The company was defense-related, with development on an internal network. It was tedious: even simple file imports and exports required burning discs, approvals, endless hassle.

The team lead was something of a dilettante—older, politically savvy, well-liked by the second-in-command. He didn't know much but loved to micromanage, often Googling something random and telling us to build it.

My colleagues were easy to get along with. Most were fresh graduates, and we could chat freely—people from Chaoshan, Henan, Hebei.

## Leaving and looking back

I left Beijing later for various reasons. There was no grand farewell—just checking out. After that, 2023 truly became "that year."

I still think of Beijing's weather, certain streets I walked often, certain shops I frequented, or those late nights leaving the office and looking up at the sky. These fragments together form my memories of that year in Beijing—nothing dramatic, but real, concrete, and worth writing down.

No matter how I write, I can't capture everything. Some memories are destined to fade, and this post is no exception. Someday it will vanish from the hard drive.
